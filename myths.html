Learning Myths

Below are some popular statements that we often see online on learning. Click on each card to see if it's true or not.

People learn best when taught in their preferred learning style.

False. Research shows no evidence that tailoring teaching to learning styles improves outcomes (Dunlosky et al., 2013).

Re-reading your notes is the best way to study.

False. Retrieval practice and testing yourself are far more effective for long-term learning (Roediger & Butler, 2011)

We only use 10% of our brain while learning.

False. All areas are active at different times during daily activities (Veerakone, 2024).

Teachers should motivate students by praising them "for being smart."

False. This kind of praise is countereffective (Mueller & Dweck, 1998). Praising effort, rather than ability, is far more likely to motivate students to work hard and improve.

Teachers don't need to know a subject area such as math or science, as long as they have good instructional skills.

False. Research shows that expertise in a subject is a key element in helping students learn better (Monk, 1994).

Intelligence is fixed at birth.

False. Intelligence doesn't solely depend on genetics (Bartels et al., 2002). Abilities can change through learning and practice.

Studying at the same place as you take the test could make you do better on the test.

True. Being in similar environments will help you retrieve better (Golden & Baddeley, 1975).

Listening to music while studying sometimes harms your efficiency.

True. There has been mixed literature on the effect of listening to music during studying. Listening to music while studying sometimes harms, especially for more difficult tasks (Ferreri & Verga, 2016)

Cramming works well if you put in enough hours.

False. Spaced practice is much more effective for long-term retention than cramming (Bahrick et al., 1993).
